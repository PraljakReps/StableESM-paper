\section{Methods}
\subsection{Curating the Stable Mutant Preference Dataset}\label{sec:dataset_curation}

We constructed the preference alignment dataset by combining experimental stability measurements from three complementary databases: MegaScale~\citep{tsuboyama2023mega}, Human Domainome 1~\citep{beltran2025site}, and FireProtDB~\citep{stourac2021fireprotdb}.
Each database contributes distinct coverage of the protein stability landscape.

\paragraph{Data sources.}
MegaScale provides large-scale protein folding stability measurements ($\Delta G$) across $\sim$100 protein domains obtained via a high-throughput proteolysis-based assay.
Human Domainome 1 provides site-saturation mutagenesis fitness measurements (growth rate-based) across 522 human protein domain libraries, totaling $\sim$563{,}000 single-point mutations.
FireProtDB is a manually curated database of thermodynamic stability data ($\Delta\Delta G$, $\Delta T_m$) linked to PDB structures, containing proteins with longer sequences (254--1{,}022 amino acids) than the other two sources.

\paragraph{Stability classification.}
For each protein domain, we classified single-point mutations as \textit{stable} or \textit{unstable} relative to the wild-type sequence.
A mutation was labeled stable if its measured fitness (or $\Delta G$) was greater than or equal to the wild-type value, and unstable otherwise.
For MegaScale, $\Delta G$ values reported as ``$>$5'' (outside the assay dynamic range) were treated as 5.0~kcal/mol.
Multi-site mutations (insertions, deletions, and double mutants) were excluded from the main training set; double mutants from MegaScale were retained separately for out-of-distribution evaluation.

\paragraph{Preference pair construction.}
For DPO training, each preference sample is a triplet $(x_{\text{wt}}, y_w, y_l)$ consisting of the wild-type sequence, a stable (preferred) mutant, and an unstable (dispreferred) mutant from the same protein domain.
Preference pairs were constructed by taking all pairwise combinations of stable and unstable mutations within each domain.
The stable and unstable mutations in a given pair need not occur at the same sequence position---the DPO objective operates on per-position log-likelihood ratios at each respective mutation site.

\paragraph{Train/validation split.}
We performed an intra-domain split to prevent information leakage: for each protein domain, 80\% of stable and unstable mutations were assigned to training and 10\% to validation, with preference pairs formed only within each split.
This ensures that no specific mutation appears in both training and validation sets, though both sets share the same set of 1{,}154 wild-type protein domains.

\paragraph{Sequence length filtering.}
All sequences were filtered to a maximum length of 256 amino acids to match the ESM-2 tokenizer input constraints used during training.
This filtering excluded the majority of FireProtDB entries (which contain longer sequences); these were instead reserved as an out-of-distribution benchmark for evaluating generalization to longer proteins (Section~\ref{sec:single_mutation_benchmark}).

\paragraph{Out-of-distribution holdouts.}
To evaluate in-distribution generalization at the protein family level, we held out specific Human Domainome domains from two PFAM families (PF00018 and PF07525) as out-of-distribution test sets.
These held-out domains share family membership with training domains but represent distinct proteins, enabling assessment of homolog-level generalization.

\paragraph{Final dataset statistics.}
The curated dataset comprises 1{,}154 unique protein domains spanning both MegaScale and Human Domainome sources.
The training set contains 184{,}938 unique stable mutants and 730{,}323 unique unstable mutants (915{,}261 total unique mutations), yielding 143{,}578{,}400 preference pairs.
The validation set contains 21{,}679 stable and 82{,}306 unstable unique mutants (103{,}985 total), yielding 1{,}824{,}750 preference pairs.
The out-of-distribution holdout from Human Domainome PF00018 and PF07525 families contains 421{,}641 preference pairs.
Only single-point mutations were used for training; the complete dataset statistics and curation pipeline are summarized in Figure~\ref{fig:overview}c.

\subsection{Benchmarking StableESM on disorder region prediction}\label{sec:disorder_prediction}
To evaluate whether stability-aligned representations transfer to unrelated protein properties, we benchmarked StableESM on per-residue disorder region prediction using the CAID (Critical Assessment of Intrinsic Disorder) benchmark. We trained on the DisProt training set and evaluated on CAID test proteins (584 proteins). For each backbone (StableESM, ESM2, ESM$_{Therm}$, one-hot encoding, and random initialization), we froze the backbone representations and trained a single linear layer ($\text{Linear}(d_{\text{hidden}}, 1)$) applied per residue to predict disorder probability. Training used binary cross-entropy loss with attention masking to ignore padding tokens, a learning rate of $1 \times 10^{-4}$, batch size of 32, maximum sequence length of 1024, and mixed precision (FP16) training for up to 1000 epochs. Performance was quantified using per-protein F1 score, accuracy, precision, and recall, aggregated across all CAID benchmark proteins.

For solubility prediction, we used the same linear probing framework with mean pooling over per-residue representations to produce a single sequence-level prediction. Training used binary cross-entropy loss, a learning rate of $1 \times 10^{-4}$, batch size of 128, and the same maximum sequence length and precision settings. Performance was quantified using AUROC, F1 score, accuracy, precision, and recall on the held-out test set.

\subsection{Testing catastrophic forgetting on structure prediction}\label{sec:structure_forgetting}
To assess whether StableESM exhibits catastrophic forgetting of structural information relative to ESM, we evaluated its learned representations using the structure module of ESMFold. Specifically, embeddings and attention maps extracted from StableESM were passed directly into the ESMFold structure module trained for 270k update steps, following the evaluation protocol used in the original ESMFold study. We evaluated models with 8M, 35M, 150M, 650M, and 3B parameters. For each model size, embeddings and attention tensors were extracted consistently with the representations used in the original ESMFold. These representations were provided as input to the ESMFold structure module, which was kept frozen throughout all experiments. No recycling steps were performed during structure prediction. Structural predictions were generated for protein targets from the CAMEO, CASP14, CASP15, and CASP16 benchmark datasets. Model performance was quantified using average per sequence pLDDT scores. 

To compare StableESM against conventional finetuning with a task-specific predictor head, we additionally evaluated representations from ESMTherm models. ESMTherm models were trained using the original ESMTherm training code and datasets and employ supervised finetuning with an explicit stability predictor head. We evaluated ESMTherm models with matching parameter sizes and extracted embeddings and attention tensors from the same attentions and representations as done for ESM and StableESM. This enabled a controlled comparison of how DPO-based optimization versus predictor head finetuning affects retention of structural signal in learned representations.

\subsection{Testing catastrophic forgetting on clinical variant prediction}\label{sec:clinical_forgetting}
\subsection{Testing catastrophic forgetting on ProteinGym benchmarks}\label{sec:proteingym_forgetting}
To further assess catastrophic forgetting in a functional prediction setting, we benchmarked StableESM representations on protein fitness prediction using the ProteinGym benchmark. Following the methodology introduced in ESM, fitness scores were computed using a log-likelihood ratio (LLR)–based metric derived from masked language modeling probabilities.

We evaluated ESM, StableESM, and ESMTherm models with 8M, 35M, 150M, 650M, and 3B parameters. For each model, predicted fitness scores were compared against experimentally measured fitness values across ProteinGym protein mutant datasets. Performance was quantified using Spearman’s rank correlation coefficient (ρ), computed separately for each dataset.

\subsection{Designing multicopper oxidase alleles and variants with StableESM}\label{sec:oxidase_design}
To validate StableESM predictions, we experimentally tested designed mutations in laccase (CotA) from \textit{Bacillus subtilis} (UniProtKB accession P07788), a protein family not represented in the preference alignment dataset. We selected mutations based on StableESM predictions using the machine learning-directed evolution framework described in Algorithm~\ref{alg:ml_directed_evolution}, including overlap variants where both StableESM and ESM2 predict stabilizing effects and StableESM-specific predictions. Enzyme activity was assessed through ABTS oxidation assays at multiple temperatures (25$^\circ$C, 65$^\circ$C, and 85$^\circ$C). All experiments were performed in quadruplicate.

For large-scale in silico design validation, we applied machine learning-directed evolution on multicopper oxidase (PDB: 1GSK), a 513 amino acid protein not represented in the preference alignment dataset. We evaluated three design strategies: (1) StableESM greedy sampling, selecting positions with highest predicted stabilizing effects; (2) StableESM unique sampling, targeting positions where StableESM predicts stabilizing mutations but ESM2 does not (LLR$_{\text{StableESM}} \geq 0$ and LLR$_{\text{ESM2}} < 0$); and (3) ESM2 greedy sampling as baseline. Thermal stability was evaluated using TempBERTura~\citep{rodella2024temberture}, and structural quality was assessed using ColabFold with mean pLDDT scores.

\subsection{Experimental protocol for protein purification}\label{sec:protein_purification}

\subsubsection{Cloning and preparing DNA constructs}
Laccase (CotA) from \textit{Bacillus subtilis} (UniProtKB accession P07788) was codon-optimized for \textit{Escherichia coli}, synthesized as a gBlock (Twist Bioscience, South San Francisco, CA, USA), and cloned into pY71 vector using Gibson Assembly. Site-directed mutations (A296G, G270C, I368V, and T261F) were introduced using the Q5\textsuperscript{\textregistered} Site-Directed Mutagenesis Kit (NEB, E0554S) with pY71-Laccase as the template. All constructs were verified by DNA sequencing.

\subsubsection{Cell-free expression and protein quantification}
Cell-free expression (CFE) reactions were performed using the PUREfrex2.0 system at 30$^\circ$C for 20 hours, and the concentration of the expressed protein was determined by Bradford assay (ThermoFisher). Protein standards (0, 0.25, 0.5, 1, 1.5, 2 mg/mL) were prepared and used as the standard curve. The absorbance at 595 nm was measured after mixing 4 $\mu$L of protein standards with 200 $\mu$L of Bradford reagent and allowing it to react at room temperature for 20 min. The protein concentration in each CFE reaction was determined from the standard curve, using no DNA as a blank control. Each sample was measured in four replicates.

\subsubsection{Copper activation and heat treatment}
Since laccase is activated by copper, we added copper sulfate (CuSO$_4$) into the CFE reaction after 20 hours of incubation. Specifically, 5 mM of CuSO$_4$ was added to the CFE system to reach a final concentration of 400 $\mu$M and incubated at room temperature (25$^\circ$C) for 2 hours in a pH 5.5 buffer environment (50 mM PIPES, 20 mM NaCl). After copper activation, each laccase mutant was subjected to heat treatment at three temperatures (85$^\circ$C, 65$^\circ$C, 25$^\circ$C) for 10 minutes to assess thermostability.

\subsection{Experimental protocol for activity assays}\label{sec:activity_assays}
The multicopper oxidase activity was measured using a colorimetric assay of 2,2'-azino-bis(3-ethylbenzothiazoline-6-sulfonic acid) (ABTS) oxidation, which turns blue upon oxidation. Colorimetric assays were performed in a pH 5.5 buffer environment (50 mM PIPES, 20 mM NaCl) containing 4 mM ABTS. The absorbance at 420 nm was measured once per minute for 30 minutes. Each sample was measured in four replicates.
